title: "The Apathy of Optimism"
description: ""
date: "Nov 24 2025"
---

I recently noticed a particular attitude towards the world, a quasi-philosophy, common in friends and family of the (neo)liberal persuasion. It is, to put it succinctly, the belief that things will always work out.

“Things will always work out” is an aphorism, more often present as a muted undertone than verbalized explicitly. It’s part of a larger collective of aphorisms that you’d find on motivational posters that go for $25 on Etsy, but it also finds pointed use in, for instance, calming down a friend who just missed their flight or bombed an exam. Part of its prevalence as an undertone is due to simple pragmatism. The belief that things are not as bad as they seem and that it will work itself out is a useful trick of the light when navigating the banalities of everyday life.

Of course, life is not always banal, and this is a dangerous belief to hold when conflict arises. Implicit in the framing of “things will always work out” is “...regardless of what you do”. It is a belief that spurs inaction, that enthusiastically neuters the rage needed to catalyze change. It is a belief intertwined in the just-world fallacy, almost liturgical in its nature. It is a belief that goes beyond acceptance of the status quo, but insists on its eternal self-propagation. No wonder then, is it a core tenet of neoliberal worldview. The myth of eternal progress that instills in you a virulent apathy.

Conflict here refers to both the personal and political: everything from life-altering events or individual injustices, to wider-scale oppression and persecution. The distinction, while ethically flimsy, is important: people compartmentalize their beliefs, and inaction in one circumstance is not always indicative. Regardless, this optimistic apathy is dangerous all the same, and manifests in many forms. At its most individualistic, it morphs into the feel-good shallowness of “accepting yourself”, acting as anathema to self-actualization and real growth. On the wider scale, it is often accompanied by a deep incuriosity about the world and its workings. It should not be left unsaid that this perspective can only really be maintained by the privileged, for those whom conflict comes rarely and never too visibly to ignore.

This is sometimes a form of resignation, an acceptance that change is impossible and a pivot to self-preservation. Given the state of powerlessness and alienation inherent under capitalism, I’d argue this is the most sympathetic of the belief’s manifestations. Regardless, putting a cheery spin on “we’re all fucked and we can’t really do anything about it so why bother” does not make it any less juvenile of an idea. Nor do I support the antithetical perspective, of pessimism or cynicism. To be perpetually angry at the world is an unsustainable state of being, and cynicism imbues its own form of apathy.

The only viable alternative is hope. A revolutionary hope that precludes apathy, for it is an active hope, of change by our own hands. In light of this, I personally employ a simple modification to the aphorism whenever I use it: “we’ll make things work out.”
